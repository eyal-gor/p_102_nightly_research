# TSM — Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company

**Sector:** Semiconductors — contract chip manufacturing (foundry)
**Market cap:** ~$1.0–1.1 trillion (June 2026, approximate)
**Added:** 2026-06-14
**Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14
**Score:** 7/10 — Exceptional, near-monopoly business at the leading edge, but priced like the market knows it and carrying real geopolitical tail risk.

## What they do
TSMC makes chips for other companies. It doesn't design them and mostly doesn't sell anything under its own brand — it runs the world's most advanced factories ("fabs") and manufactures the physical silicon that Apple, Nvidia, AMD, Qualcomm and others design. Think of it as the world's best print shop, except the "printing" is etching transistors a few nanometers wide, and almost nobody else on Earth can do it at the cutting edge. Customers pay for wafers (the silicon discs chips are cut from), and the most advanced process nodes command the highest prices and fattest margins.

The money comes from selling manufacturing capacity, and the economics are unusually good for a hardware business: gross margins in the mid-50s percent, very high returns on capital, and pricing power that grows as fewer rivals can keep up. The catch is that staying ahead costs enormous money — TSMC spends tens of billions a year building new fabs and buying the EUV lithography machines (from ASML) needed for each generation. So it's a capital monster, but one that has compounded that capital at high rates because demand for leading-edge chips — phones, then data-center GPUs for AI — keeps showing up.

## Why interesting
This is close to a true natural monopoly on the most valuable manufacturing process in the modern economy. At the bleeding edge (3nm and below), TSMC is essentially the only credible supplier — Intel has stumbled for years and Samsung's yields lag. The AI buildout makes its best customers (Nvidia especially) desperate for capacity, which strengthens TSMC's pricing hand. When the entire AI economy physically runs on chips you and only you can make, that's about as wide a moat as exists in hardware.

## Risks
The obvious one is Taiwan. The vast majority of TSMC's most advanced production sits ~100 miles from mainland China, and a conflict or blockade wouldn't just dent earnings — it would be a global catastrophe and could wipe out the thesis. The Arizona and Japan fabs help diversify but are expensive and won't replace Taiwan's scale for years. Beyond geopolitics: it's cyclical (chip demand swings hard), capex keeps rising, the AI demand could prove to be a bubble that's over-ordered capacity, and as a foreign company it carries currency and US-listing/ADR quirks. At today's valuation a lot of good news is already priced in.

## History

### 2026-06-14 — first look
What stands out is the combination of monopoly-grade moat at the leading edge and the single biggest concentration risk in global business — almost everything depends on one island. The business quality is a clear 9–10; the score lands at 7 because the price isn't cheap after the AI run-up and the Taiwan tail risk is unhedgeable and binary. It's the kind of name you want to own at a fearful moment, not necessarily after a euphoric one.
