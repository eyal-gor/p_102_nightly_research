# ASML — ASML Holding N.V.

**Sector:** Semiconductor capital equipment (lithography systems)
**Market cap:** ~$320B (June 2026, approximate)
**Added:** 2026-06-14
**Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14
**Score:** 7/10 — a near-perfect monopoly business, but cyclical, politically exposed, and rarely cheap

## What they do
ASML makes the machines that print the circuitry onto computer chips. The chip itself is made by companies like TSMC, Samsung, and Intel — but to draw those impossibly tiny patterns, they need lithography machines, and ASML is the only company on Earth that sells the most advanced kind: EUV (extreme ultraviolet) lithography. These are room-sized machines that cost roughly $150–400M each, take a fleet of suppliers to build (notably Zeiss for the optics), and ship only a few hundred a year. If you want to make the leading-edge chips that go into AI accelerators, phones, and data centers, you essentially have to buy from ASML. There is no second source.

They make money two ways. First, selling the machines themselves — big, lumpy, expensive orders that rise and fall with the chip industry's investment cycle. Second, and increasingly important, a growing service-and-upgrade business: once a customer has a $300M machine, ASML keeps earning from maintenance, spare parts, and performance upgrades over its long life. That installed-base revenue is steadier and helps smooth out the boom-bust nature of equipment sales.

## Why interesting
This is about as close to a true monopoly as exists in a critical industry. The technical moat is real and decades deep — competitors aren't a few years behind on EUV, they basically don't exist. Demand has a strong long-term tailwind from AI compute and the broader need for more and better chips, and the high-end High-NA EUV machines give them a fresh, even pricier product cycle to sell into. When the business is good, the margins and cash generation are excellent.

## Risks
Three big ones. (1) It's cyclical — chip-equipment orders swing hard, so even a wonderful business can have ugly years, and the stock reacts violently to order trends. (2) Geopolitics — China has been a large chunk of sales, and U.S./Dutch export restrictions can be tightened at any time, cutting off a market with little warning. (3) Price — ASML is almost never "cheap"; the quality is widely understood, so you're usually paying a premium multiple, which limits the margin of safety and means a stumble can hurt the stock badly even if the long-term story is intact. Customer concentration (a handful of foundries drive most demand) adds another dependency.

## History

### 2026-06-14 — first look
What stands out is the cleanest moat in tech hardware: a sole supplier of EUV with AI-driven demand behind it and a sticky service stream growing underneath the lumpy machine sales. I'm holding the score at 7 rather than higher because the two things outside their control — the chip cycle and export politics — can dent results regardless of how good the business is, and the stock rarely trades at a price that gives you a cushion. Exceptional company, but "exceptional at a fair setup" depends heavily on where the valuation and the cycle sit when you buy.
