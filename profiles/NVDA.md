# NVDA — NVIDIA Corporation

**Sector:** Semiconductors / AI computing hardware and software
**Market cap:** ~$3.4 trillion (June 2026, approximate — moves a lot day to day)
**Added:** 2026-06-14
**Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14
**Score:** 6/10 — extraordinary business, but the price already assumes years of near-perfect execution

## What they do
NVIDIA designs the chips (GPUs) that have become the default engine for training and running AI models. They don't own factories — they design the chips and hand the manufacturing to TSMC — but they capture most of the value because their hardware is the fastest and, crucially, because nearly all AI software is written for their CUDA platform. That software lock-in is the real moat: even when a rival chip is competitive on paper, moving off CUDA is painful, so customers keep coming back. The big data-center GPUs (the H100/Blackwell-class parts and their successors) are where the money is now.

They make money mostly by selling these chips and full server systems to a handful of very large customers — Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Meta, and a wave of AI labs and "neoclouds." That's both the strength and the worry: a huge share of revenue comes from a small number of buyers who are spending enormous sums on AI infrastructure. NVIDIA also sells gaming GPUs, chips for cars and robotics, and networking gear (from the Mellanox acquisition), but data-center AI dwarfs everything else today.

## Why interesting
This is one of the genuinely rare businesses: dominant share of a market that's still growing fast, gross margins that look more like software than hardware, and a software ecosystem (CUDA) that keeps customers stuck. When a company sits at the toll booth of the single biggest technology buildout of the decade, it deserves attention regardless of price.

## Risks
The whole story rests on AI capital spending staying enormous, and that spending is concentrated in a few hyperscalers who are also building their own chips (Google's TPUs, Amazon's Trainium, etc.) to reduce dependence on NVIDIA. If AI returns disappoint, or if customers "digest" the chips they've already bought, orders can drop fast and the stock is priced for the opposite. Add real overhangs: China export restrictions, the risk that custom silicon and AMD chip away at margins over time, and a valuation that leaves little room for a stumble. The business is exceptional; the *setup* at today's price is where the skepticism belongs.

## History

### 2026-06-14 — first look
The quality is not in question — dominant position, CUDA moat, software-like margins. What holds the score down is price and concentration: the market cap already bakes in years of continued hyperscaler spending, and a small group of customers (who are actively building their own chips) drive most of the revenue. A 6 reflects "world-class business, demanding setup" — I'd want either a cheaper entry or more proof the AI capex cycle is durable before scoring it higher.
